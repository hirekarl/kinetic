# Jordan — User Persona

**Age:** 34
**Location:** Chicago (Logan Square)
**Role:** Senior Software Engineer, Series B SaaS company (~180 employees)
**Comp:** $148k base + equity — comfortable, not wealthy; no financial cushion to outsource problems away

---

## Professional Profile

Jordan has been writing code professionally for nine years. The first four were scrappy — a bootcamp, two small agencies, a startup that didn't make it. The last five have been legitimately good: a mid-size product company, real ownership, work that ships and matters.

The title is Senior but the actual job is somewhere between senior IC and informal tech lead. Jordan doesn't manage anyone on paper, but in practice trains the junior engineers, gets pulled into architecture discussions, and is the person PMs call when something is on fire and they need a straight answer. That informal load doesn't show up in the job description.

The company is growing fast enough that process hasn't caught up to headcount. Meetings have multiplied. The codebase is accumulating debt. Jordan is good enough to see the problems clearly, experienced enough to know how they compound, and not senior enough yet to slow things down without social cost.

Work starts around 8:30 and rarely ends before 7. Async communication fills the gaps.

---

## Personal Profile

Jordan lives alone in a two-bedroom apartment. The second bedroom is nominally an office, practically a storage room. He moved to Chicago three years ago for the job — left behind a tighter social infrastructure in Columbus and hasn't fully rebuilt it.

He has maybe four people he'd call close friends. Marcus is one of them — they met in a Slack community for engineers and have been in each other's orbit for two years, mostly async. They talk in bursts: a few threads close together, then weeks of silence. Jordan is aware that he's been the one letting the silence stretch.

He used to run. Did a half-marathon in 2022 and genuinely enjoyed training for it. That version of him feels like someone else right now. The running shoes are by the door.

He cooks when he has the energy, orders out when he doesn't, and can't always remember which one he did more of this week. Laundry is the canary — when it piles up for more than five days, something else is wrong too. It's a reliable signal.

He doesn't think of himself as burned out. He thinks of himself as behind.

---

## The Gap Kinetic Fills

Jordan is not disorganized. He has a task manager (Things 3), a sleep tracker (Oura ring, bought last year, mostly used to confirm he slept badly), and a rough weekly review habit he maintains about two weeks out of four. He has the tools. He doesn't have synthesis.

Every morning he opens three separate things — the task manager, the health app, a mental model of his social situation — and tries to do triage in his head. That process takes 30 to 45 minutes, consumes the clearest part of his cognitive day, and still produces a ranked list he doesn't fully trust.

What Jordan actually wants is a single answer to one question: **given where I am right now, what are the two or three things that will do the most work if I do them today?** Not a list of everything. Not a dashboard to interpret. A prioritized call to action, already reasoned through, ready to execute.

He would check in every morning if it took 30 seconds. He would not fill out a form. He would not open five different apps. He would send a message, the same way he'd brief a colleague, and expect a useful response.

---

## What Jordan Sounds Like in a Check-in

> "I'm completely overwhelmed. Four hours sleep, laundry six days overdue, haven't talked to Marcus in weeks."

That's it. No categories, no metadata, no effort to be precise. Just a status report the way you'd give it to someone you trust to figure out what it means.
